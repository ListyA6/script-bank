# Client didn't know their own workflow

> Premise: Listy takes on a client project to automate their operations but can't systemize it because every staff member does the job differently and nobody — including the owner — can tell him the actual process. The project stalls because there's no single truth to build on.

## Hooks — write 5+, pick 1

1. I spent three weeks on a client project and I still don't know what the real process is.
2. *(chosen)* A client hired me to build their system. I just couldn't figure out what their system actually was.
3. Everyone on the team did the job a different way. And nobody thought that was a problem.
4. The hardest software project I've had this year had nothing to do with the software.
5. I asked five staff how they do the same task. I got five different answers.
6. The owner couldn't tell me how their own business works. And they'd been running it for two years.

## Voiceover — clean read
<!-- The full spoken track, exactly the way it's said. Read it aloud once. -->

A client hired me to build their system. I just couldn't figure out what their system actually was.

They had inventory, orders, a small team — maybe eight people. Owner said the process was simple. So I talked to the staff. First person does it one way. Second person does it different. Third person, completely different again. And when I asked the owner, she kind of described a fourth version.

But here's what made it stall.

There was no single version anyone agreed on. Not written down, not even in anyone's head. The business ran because the staff were flexible enough to fill the gaps. But you can't build software on flexible. Software needs a rule. One rule. And nobody could give me one.

I went back to the owner three times. And each time she'd say, honestly, just do what feels right. Which I get — that's how she's been running it. But that's not something I can write into a system. So the project's sitting.

Three weeks in. Still no spec.

## Notes
- **Pronunciation flags:** "inventory" — stress on first syllable (IN-ven-to-ry), fine for him. "Flexible" — watch the /fl/ cluster, deliver slowly. "Described" — final /d/ may drop, that's fine. "Completely" — 4 syllables, keep it or swap for "totally" if it trips.
- **Music / SFX:** low ambient hum or silence works — no energetic beat, this is a stall story. Subtle tension underscore if anything.
- **Delivery note:** the close "Three weeks in. Still no spec." should be flat and slightly tired — not punchy, not sarcastic. Just the fact.
- **Loop check:** hook opens "I couldn't figure out what their system was" — payoff resolves it at "that's not something I can write into a system." Close ends on a concrete count and state, not a lesson.
