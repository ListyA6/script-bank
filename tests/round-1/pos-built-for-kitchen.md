# POS built kitchen-first

> Listy argues that every standard POS is built for the cashier, so the kitchen — where orders actually break — gets no information and no control. He builds POS the other way around: kitchen screen first, cashier second.

## Hooks — write 5+, pick 1
1. Every POS I've seen is built for the cashier. But the cashier doesn't lose orders. The kitchen does.
2. Most restaurant software is designed for the person taking your money, not the person making your food.
3. I've run three kitchens. The cashier never loses an order. The kitchen loses it every time.
4. The default POS puts a big screen at the front. The kitchen gets a printed ticket and a prayer.
5. If your kitchen is slow, it's probably not the cooks. It's what they can't see.
6. I built a POS backwards compared to every off-the-shelf system. The kitchen gets the screen. The cashier gets the terminal.

## Voiceover — clean read
<!-- The full spoken track, exactly the way it's said. Read it aloud once. -->

I've run three kitchens. The cashier never loses an order. The kitchen loses it every time.

Every standard POS puts a big screen at the front counter and sends the kitchen a printed slip. Which is fine until it's 7pm and there are thirty orders in the queue.

I watched my cooks lose track of the queue every Friday. Not because they're slow. Because the slip doesn't tell them what's waiting, what's next, or when the order came in. They're guessing.

So I built ours differently.

The kitchen screen shows the full queue, sorted by wait time, color-coded when an order's been sitting more than eight minutes. The cashier's terminal is basically just the payment side. It sends the order straight to the kitchen, and the kitchen confirms when it's done.

And here's the thing — the first week we ran it, average wait on a Friday dropped. Not because the cooks got faster. Because they stopped guessing.

Most POS software is designed by people who've stood at the front counter. I've stood in the back. That's basically where I built this from.

Still tweaking it. The eight-minute threshold is probably wrong for the noodle shop — different cook times.
