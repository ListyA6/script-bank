# 1,200 plates a week taught me something no dashboard can show you

> Premise: Running a high-volume kitchen at scale trained Listy to read real-time ops by feel — throughput, where the line slows, which station is about to break — and he's never found a SaaS metric that replicates that.

2. No software tells you a bottleneck is coming. But a kitchen does.

4. I used to think the data was the operation. Then I worked the floor.

6. The most useful ops dashboard I have is the sound of a kitchen at 7pm.

## Voiceover — clean read
<!-- The full spoken track, exactly the way it's said. Read it aloud once. -->

I run 1,200 plates a week. And it taught me something no SaaS dashboard ever could.

You can have all the charts you want — daily covers, ticket times, table turns. I had those. They didn't tell me a Friday night was about to fall apart.

The kitchen tells you before the numbers do. One station goes quiet when it should be loud, the line slows by maybe three plates and nobody's called it yet — but I feel it. That's the bottleneck starting.

And I only know that because I stood there for months before I built anything.

I started building our order routing system after working the floor. Not before. Because once you've felt the difference between 80 covers moving and 80 covers stuck, you stop solving the wrong thing. The ticket time going up isn't the problem — it's the symptom. The problem is always one specific station, one missing prep step, one person who didn't get the briefing.

No dashboard I've found yet shows you that in real time. Not at 7pm when it matters.

So now the software I build for kitchens has alerts that fire on rate of change, not on the final number. Because by the time the final number is bad, you're already forty plates behind.

I still walk the floor every Friday before service. The app can't do that part yet.
