# My family still thinks this isn't real work

> Listy grew up in a family that valued physical, tangible work. Two years ago he started posting seriously — it still feels new and a little uncomfortable.

## Hooks — write 5+, pick 1

1. My family has never understood what I do for work. And honestly, two years in, I'm still not sure I can explain it.
2. I started posting two years ago because I needed to. But I kept thinking my family would see it and think I'd given up.
3. I grew up watching my dad come home dirty. That's what work looked like. And then I started making videos on my phone.
4. There's this thing where I post a video and then immediately feel like I should be doing something else. Something real.
5. I still feel a little weird telling people I post on social media. Which is strange because I also run three restaurants and build software for a living.
6. Two years ago I made my first serious post. My hands were fine. That was kind of the problem.

## Voiceover — clean read
<!-- The full spoken track, exactly the way it's said. Read it aloud once. -->

I still feel a little weird telling people I post on social media. Which is strange because I also run three restaurants and build software for a living. But I grew up in a house where work meant something you could hold. My dad came home with dirt on him. That meant the day was real. And for a long time I measured every day against that. So when I started posting, two years ago, it felt like cheating. I'd finish a shoot and then go wash dishes just to feel like I'd done something. I'm not joking. And I'd post the video and then sit there thinking, okay but what did I actually make? You can't hold a reel. But here's the thing I keep noticing. The restaurants run the same whether I post or not. But new clients come in asking for software, and they say they found me here. So I think the discomfort is just old calibration. Same loop, different surface. I still feel it though. I finish a video and look at my hands and think, okay, what else.
