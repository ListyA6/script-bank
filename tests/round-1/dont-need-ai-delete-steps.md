# You don't need AI. Delete the steps first.

> A business tries to automate a broken workflow instead of removing the steps that shouldn't exist. Listy shows the pattern from his own kitchen and software builds — the first move is always subtraction, not addition.

## Hooks — write 5+, pick 1

1. You don't need AI. You need to delete three steps.
2. Everyone's asking me to add automation. I keep telling them to delete things first.
3. I spent two months automating a process that should've just stopped existing.
4. The most expensive thing I build is the system someone should've just removed.
5. Most businesses want a faster broken process. That's not the same as a fixed one.
6. Before I write a single line of code, I ask: which steps here shouldn't exist at all?

## Voiceover — clean read
<!-- The full spoken track, exactly the way it's said. Read it aloud once. -->

Everyone's asking me to add automation. I keep telling them to delete things first.

Because most of the time when I map out a client's workflow, there are steps in there that exist only because someone never questioned them.

I had this at one of my own kitchens. Order comes in, staff writes it on paper, moves it to a second paper, photos it, sends the photo to the cook. Four steps. The cook still missed orders.

So I built a screen.

Not to automate the paper process. To replace it. Three of those four steps are gone now. The cook sees the order the second it's placed, no handoff, no photo, no delay. That kitchen runs with two fewer people on the floor during lunch.

But here's where most people get it wrong. They come to me and say "I want to automate my workflow" and what they mean is, make the broken version faster.

So before I touch code I ask them to walk me through every step and tell me why it exists. Usually three of them don't have an answer. Those three go. Then we build.

Sometimes the whole project gets smaller. Honestly, that's the point.

## Post caption + hashtags

Most businesses don't need automation. They need to remove the steps that shouldn't exist.

I've been building custom software for restaurants and small businesses for a few years now and the pattern is always the same — someone wants to speed up a broken process instead of cutting the broken part out.

The first question I ask before writing any code: which of these steps would disappear if you started from scratch today?

Usually three of them have no answer.
