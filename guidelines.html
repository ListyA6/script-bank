Script Guidelines.

How you write a script that sounds like Listy. Everything here got boiled down from the 13 research notes, so if you only read one file before writing, read this. The notes are the evidence. This is the law.

When in doubt, read the line out loud at talking speed and ask: would a tired 28-year-old who runs restaurants and writes software actually say this? Or did a content calendar?

Who he is

So the voice has a spine.

Listy. 28, Indonesian, male. Runs an agency that builds custom automation software for real businesses. POS, inventory, booking, order systems, all tuned to one company's actual workflow. He also runs restaurants (LalaFun ayam geprek, Floural bread, Kosolo noodles), and he's killed ventures too. SaraFun, closed. Before all of this he ran a design / branding / web agency.

His edge over a normal dev is simple: he's felt the operational pain himself. He's lived the kitchen, the payroll, the supplier, the 7pm queue. So when he systemizes something, it's the actual bottleneck, not the one a developer imagines from a desk. He can build for studio bookings, a bakery, a noodle shop, because he sits with the operator until he finds the real friction.

✓ Positioning

Operator who builds, not authority. He's somewhere between 3 months and a year in, so he's not a teacher on a stage. He's a guy reporting from the field. Camera-shy too, which is why it's mostly voiceover + B-roll. And this is a personal-brand channel. The businesses are proof and fuel, but the subject is him, building and running things in public.

1

The non-negotiables ★

Break one and the script is dead. Read every draft line by line against these seven.

Never speak a rupiah / revenue figure out loud.

Money lives on the screen as a proof shot, never in the voiceover. The only numbers his mouth says are counts and dates. Plates, staff, months, "the 11th," "three days." Saying your own revenue out loud is flex-y and weird, and he hates it. (See §4.)

No crafted closing line. Just stop.

Real reels don't button the ending. End on the last real thing that happened, said plainly, and the video stops. If the last line feels a little unfinished, good, that means it's right. (See §6 — the rule most drafts break.)

Don't state the lesson.

End on the thing and let the viewer make the point themselves. The moral should land in their head half a second after the video ends, never from his mouth.

No guru words.

Banned:

synergyleveragescale to 7 figuresunlocktransformationjourneyecosystemdisruptivevalue-addgame-changingnext-levellevel upcrush itdream biggermindset
No AI tells.

No em dashes, use a period or a comma instead. No "it's not X, it's Y" as a close, and at most once anywhere inside a script. No perfect parallel triplets. No essay transitions like "with that in mind," "moving on to," "it's worth noting."

Every claim gets an object, a number, or a date.

Adjectives don't carry weight here, specifics do. "It took a while" becomes "three months." "It did well" becomes show the screen.

Don't chop it into staccato.

"One offer. Zero clients. That's it." is its own AI tell. The 4–9 word target is about clauses, so chain them into real sentences with and / so / because / which is why. And vary the length hard. (See §5.)

2

Voice fingerprint

How Listy actually talks, built from his own words. These patterns separate "sounds like him" from "sounds like the generic builder guy."

  • Statement, then the complication. His natural unit is declaration + but/so/except. The complication is the honest part. Once I start, it rolls. But starting is hard. Declaration = hook, complication = re-hook.
  • Name the loop in real time. He steps back and labels the pattern he's stuck in while he's in it. It's looping. My stupid brain delays it to the perfect time. Which never comes. His strongest move. The mid-video pull-back where he names the thing the viewer is stuck in too.
  • Correction mid-sentence, not after. He revises his reasoning inside the sentence. I think I'm avoiding failure. Not sure that's the real reason. Write the confident version, then add one short deflating clause. One. Not a paragraph of hedging.
  • "It feels like" is a specific tool, not filler. He uses it to mark something he doesn't fully understand yet, vs. "I think" when he has a theory. Don't swap one for the other.
  • Dry self-criticism, then the mechanic. "My brain does this. Here's what I do about it." Never "it's been such a hard journey." Quick, dry, move on.
  • Analogy through lived systems, not metaphor. Show the concrete thing, then label it. Miss 7am, wait for 7:30, then 8, then tomorrow. ADHD logic.
  • Numbers ground a cost or a decision, never status. The number attaches to the object, not to him. LalaFun, the busy one, never "my 130-juta restaurant."
  • Doubt without retreat. He states the fear, measures it, continues anyway. Worst case? One more failure in the bucket. Named, sized, then walked past. It never cancels the action.
  • Hedges are semantic, not weak. "basically," "kind of," "pretty much" mark an approximation. Keep them on approximations. They make him sound honest, not unsure.

Structure & pacing

The 45s spine. Default 45s (~95–130 words). 30s = 65–75 words. 60s = 130–145 words.

BeatTimeWhat it does
Hook0–3sA claim, a number on screen, or the thing itself. Open a loop. Face, tight.
Context3–8sWhy it matters / the situation. Cut to B-roll, voice continues.
Body A8–15sFirst concrete proof point. Not abstract.
Re-hook15–17s"But here's where it broke." Snap to face ~2s. Fights the 15s cliff.
Body B17–28sSecond proof / the twist. Different B-roll than A.
Re-hook 228–32sFace return ~2–3s, slower. Sets up the resolve. Fights the 30s drift.
Payoff32–40sResolve the opening loop. One concrete line.
Close40–45sStop on the last real thing. No crafted button. (See §6.)
  • Open a loop in the first sentence and don't resolve it until ~70–80%. If the hook gives away the payoff there's no loop, and they're gone by second 8.
  • Pacing envelope: rapid → slow → rapid. Tight cuts 0–8s, let the proof breathe 8–28s (2–3s holds), then bring the energy back 28–45s. New creators hold intro-energy the whole way through and it's exhausting to watch.
  • Re-hooks have to carry new information. A face-return at 30s that says nothing makes the viewer feel tricked.
4

The numbers rule

Read this twice. It's the rule that gets broken the most, and the one Listy cares about most.

  • Rupiah and revenue never appear in the spoken track. They live on screen as a proof shot. A POS day-total, a Stripe chart, GoFood orders, a bank notification, with the key number circled. The voice keeps talking underneath and never announces the figure.
  • In the voice, scale gets words, not digits. "My busiest shop." "The small one." "The kitchen that supplies the other two." Never "my 130-juta restaurant."
  • The only spoken numbers are counts and dates. "Fourteen people." "1,200 plates a day." "Three months." "Closed it on the 7th." "I've got three days." Those don't read as flex.
  • When a number has to be shown, frame it as a cost or a responsibility, not a win. Pair it with payroll, rent, tax. "This pays 14 people" lands. "We did 130 juta" flexes. Let the viewer do the bragging in the comments.
  • Round down, never up. "About 130," not "131.4." And on screen, real and slightly messy (other tabs visible, a real timestamp) beats a clean mocked PNG. A screenshot with no visible source now reads as negative proof.
5

Spoken register

Write for the mouth, not the page. The unit is a 2–7 word clause, but those clauses are chained into real sentences. Both halves matter.

  • Contract everything. it's, I'm, don't, that's, here's. Nobody says "do not" out loud.
  • Open sentences with And / So / But / Now. That's ~15% of real sentence starts, and it does the job "furthermore / however / therefore" do in writing, minus the stiffness.
  • Zero formal connectors. howeverthereforefurthermoremoreoverthushenceneverthelessin conclusion These show up exactly zero times in 14,000 words of real viral speech. Use But / So / And / Now instead.
  • Chain, then snap. Run a long messy clause-chain ("...to the point that I hired someone whose whole job is just X"), then cut to something short. Vary the length hard. Every line clipped is just as wrong as all run-ons.
  • Active voice always. "Early investors outperform," not "it has been shown that..."
  • One rhetorical question max, right before the key point or at the hook. Then answer it. Never leave it hanging for drama.
  • Light discourse markers welcome: honestly, look, basically, kind of, pretty much, man. One internet-voice phrase per video max (it just works / nobody cares / ship it).
6

Endings

The make-or-break. Listy's #1 complaint about drafts: closes that "try to make a point." He's right, they read as AI.

The diagnosis: an AI close is on-the-nose. It says the moral out loud and hands it to the viewer. Real people almost never button a story with its own thesis, so when a script does, it stops sounding like a person.

✓ First-choice move — don't write a close at all

End on the last real thing that happens, say it plainly, stop. Even a "natural-sounding" button ("so I started on a Thursday") is still writing a close, and it reads as AI. If the final line feels slightly unfinished, you did it right.

If you do shape an exit, pick exactly one of these six. None of them states a lesson.

  1. End on the concrete detail. Stop on the physical thing and let the meaning sit underneath. So that's my week. Writing down exactly how much salt goes in the broth.
  2. Undercut / deflate. Puncture your own seriousness. Anyway. Back to it. · I'll probably still mess it up.
  3. Trail off. We'll see if two new cooks can read my handwriting. · Ask me in December.
  4. Hard-stop on a fact. A concrete figure (count/date, never rupiah spoken) with zero commentary. Last day's the 11th. I've got a notebook and three days.
  5. Literal callback. Loop back to the opening image and say it flat. Don't reword it into a metaphor. Back to the same recipe.
  6. Understate it. Say the smaller, flatter version and let the weight stay implied. Cheaper than the next one would've been.
✕ Banned closes — read as AI every time
  • "It's not X, it's Y."
  • "I'd rather [X] than [Y]." (symmetric trade-off)
  • Stating the theme: "a business shouldn't live in one person's head."
  • Matched-pair aphorism: "The folder remembers. I just decide."
  • Ending on an abstract noun: "That's the bet." "That's discipline." "That's the timestamp."
  • Any line built to be screenshotted on a motivational graphic. If it'd look at home on a poster, kill it.

The test: read the close out loud. Is it stating the lesson or ending on a thing? If you can hear the moral being announced, rewrite to a concrete detail, an undercut, or an unfinished thread.

7

Hooks

First 3 seconds. Open on a scene or a claim. Never a logo, never an intro card, never "hey guys." Ship it in ≤3s and keep it specific. Write 5+ variants per script, then pick one.

Hook types (match hook_type in frontmatter):

receipts
number/result first, proof shot in seconds 2–4. (Number on SCREEN.)
build-reveal
"I built X in Y." Cut to the working thing.
contrarian
a take you've lived the scar tissue for.
curiosity
specific promise, withheld payoff. Payoff must be a real tactic/number.
pain-mirror
name a private fear out loud, then show the fix from your own build.
fail-story
public loss with a date and a number. Unfakeable for an operator.
bts
open on the actual screen/kitchen, not your face.
listicle
3–5 only, tactical, fast cuts.
hot-take
category-specific opinion you can defend through the whole video.
before-after
visual delta (dashboard, design, day-1 vs day-30).
✓ The rule

Show face when the hook is a claim. Cut to the screen when the hook is proof.

8

B-roll & proof

The operator's unfair advantage. Something on screen changes every 1.5–3s. And every B-roll clip earns its place by proving a claim. If it doesn't prove or punctuate, cut it.

Listy's moat: he has both dashboards and a physical ops floor. Cut between a Stripe chart and a wok firing in the same 30 seconds, because that contrast is the brand. Shoot both worlds.

Vocabulary (each says something)

  • screenshare / code = "I built this"
  • POS / metrics = authority (1–2s)
  • product-in-hand = "this is real"
  • kitchen / ops / staff = "I run things." This one's the moat.
  • phone-UI = customer experience
  • whiteboard = "I think in systems"
  • throwbacks = "I earned the right"
✓ Translate restaurant proof for a tech audience

POS daily report = "this is our Stripe." 7pm queue = the hockey stick. Repeat-order receipts = retention cohort. 14 staff at briefing = the team page. Say it out loud: You ship code. I ship 1,200 plates a day. Same loop.

The proof-shot pattern

Mid-video the talking head shrinks/freezes, a real screenshot slams full-frame with the key number circled, hold 1.5–2s, back to face. Land it on a claim, not a tangent. One key number, circled. Keep the audio talking underneath. Max two per video.

9

Captions & on-screen text

  • Default style: sentence-chunked 3–5 word phrases (builder content). Karaoke word-by-word only for a hype beat. One pinned top line = the hook restated, held 5–10s.
  • Font: one clean sans (Inter / Geist / SF Pro), locked weight+size+stroke, reused every video. Mono/tabular figures for numbers so digits don't jitter. One accent color for highlights. No emojis, no rainbow, no platform-default preset.
  • Highlight 1–2 words per phrase. Nouns, numbers, names, action verbs. Never articles.
  • Numbers are a design element: count-up on first reveal only, pair with a unit chip ("3 restaurants"), hold 1.5–2s so it can be screenshotted.
  • Bilingual: English captions always, since the builder audience is international. Indonesian VO is fine, just burn English captions and let the accent be texture. Keep dish, place and brand names in Indonesian, untranslated (ayam geprek, Kediri, warung).
10

CTA

✓ Default: no hard CTA

The work is the CTA. If the last frame already provokes "who is this guy," shut up.

  • If you use one, make it soft, conditional, identity-based, placed AFTER the payoff: If you build things on weekends, this account's for you. Never "follow now for more dev tips." One CTA max. Never read it louder than the rest.
  • Run any CTA as burned-in text during the payoff, not as a spoken last line. A spoken CTA breaks the loop.
11

ESL delivery constraints

Write words his mouth can land cleanly. The accent is Indonesian-English, so pick words that record clean on the first take.

  • Avoid final consonant clusters where a synonym exists: prefer "the price" over "the costs," "find" over "find out," "think about" over "think through." Prefer "exactly" over "exact," "we shipped it" over "shipped" as a sentence-ending stop.
  • Avoid v/f/b pile-ups in one clause. "Proven very profitable" is a tongue trap, break it up. Proper nouns starting B/P/F sit alone, not next to similar sounds.
  • Avoid 4+ syllable formal words: systematically, fundamentally, predominantly, nevertheless. Use "mostly," "a lot," "still."
  • Keep sentences short enough to deliver with correct stress. Mark the one stressed word per sentence. One high-effort word per sentence, max.

The gut-check

Run before calling a script done. Read the whole thing out loud at talking speed, then tick these off.

If it passes all 8, it's ready to shoot.

A worked example

The target. Failure pillar, fail-story hook, 45s. He closed a satellite restaurant (SaraFun).

Pillar: failure Hook: fail-story Length: 45s

Hooks (5, picked #2)

  1. I closed a restaurant this year. Here's the part nobody posts.
  2. I shut one of my shops down in April. Best money I didn't keep spending.chosen
  3. Everyone says don't quit. I quit a whole restaurant. On purpose.
  4. This shop was open four months. Then I killed it.
  5. The mistake wasn't opening it. It was opening it without one guy.

Voiceover

I shut one of my shops down in April. (beat) Four months open. Then I closed it. Not because it lost money. Because I opened it before I had the one person who runs a kitchen when I'm not there. So every problem came straight to my phone. Supplier's late, my phone. Cook quits, my phone. I was running it from a different city, which, yeah, that's on me. The main shop was fine the whole time, because that one has the guy. So I added it up, and closing was cheaper than keeping it on life support. Two cooks from the closed one moved to the main kitchen. They start Monday.

On-screen

Pinned Closed a shop. On purpose. · proof shot = the closure math / final P&L with the number circled (NOT spoken) · throwback photo of the shop's day-1.

✓ Why it works

The number lives on screen, and the voice only carries counts and dates ("four months," "April," "Monday"). The loop ("best money I didn't keep spending") resolves at "cheaper than keeping it on life support." And the close ends on a concrete fact, two cooks starting Monday, not a lesson. No "the real lesson is hire first." The viewer assembles that themselves.

✕ What would've made it AI-y

Ending on "Don't open the second shop until the kitchen can run without you." True, but on-the-nose. The script shows it and stops.
