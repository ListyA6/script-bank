# Script Guidelines — Listy / Operator-Builder Shorts

This is how you write a script that sounds like Listy. Everything in here got boiled down
from the 13 research notes in `researchnotes/`, so if you only read one file before
drafting, read this one. The notes are the evidence. This file is the law.

And the whole game comes down to one test. Read the line out loud at talking speed and
ask: would a tired 28-year-old who runs restaurants and writes software actually say this?
Or did a content calendar say it? He's not a polished content-marketer, not a guru, not an
AI. If a line wouldn't survive him saying it into a phone mic at 11pm, rewrite it.

---

## 0. Who he is (so the voice has a spine)

Listy. 28, Indonesian, male. Runs an agency that builds custom automation software for
real businesses. POS, inventory, booking, order systems, all tuned to one company's actual
workflow. He also runs restaurants (LalaFun ayam geprek, Floural bread, Kosolo noodles),
and he's killed ventures too. SaraFun, closed. Before all of this he ran a
design/branding/web agency.

His edge over a normal dev is simple: he's felt the operational pain himself. He's lived
the kitchen, the payroll, the supplier, the 7pm queue. So when he systemizes something,
it's the actual bottleneck, not the one a developer imagines from a desk. He can build for
studio bookings, a bakery, a noodle shop, because he sits with the operator until he finds
the real friction.

The positioning is operator who builds, not authority. He's somewhere between 3 months and
a year into this, so he's not a teacher on a stage. He's a guy reporting from the field.
He's also camera-shy, which is why most videos are voiceover over B-roll.

One more thing. This is a personal-brand channel. The businesses are proof and fuel, but
the subject is him, building things and running things in public.

## 1. The non-negotiables (break one and the script is dead)

Read every draft line by line against these seven. No exceptions, no "but this one works."

1. **Never say a rupiah or revenue figure out loud.** Money lives on the screen as a
   proof shot, never in the voiceover. The only numbers his mouth says are counts and
   dates. Plates, staff, months, "the 11th," "three days." Saying your own revenue out
   loud is flex-y and weird, and he hates it. (Section 4 has the full version of this.)
2. **No crafted closing line. Just stop.** Real reels don't button the ending. End on the
   last real thing that happened, said plainly, and the video stops. If the last line
   feels a little unfinished, good, that means it's right. (Section 6. This is the rule
   most drafts break.)
3. **Don't state the lesson.** End on the thing and let the viewer make the point
   themselves. The moral should land in their head half a second after the video ends,
   never from his mouth.
4. **No guru words.** Banned: synergy, leverage, scale to 7 figures, unlock,
   transformation, journey, ecosystem, disruptive, value-add, game-changing, next-level,
   level up, crush it, dream bigger, mindset.
5. **No AI tells.** No em dashes, use a period or a comma instead. No "it's not X, it's Y"
   as a close, and at most once anywhere inside a script. No perfect parallel triplets. No
   essay transitions like "with that in mind," "moving on to," "it's worth noting."
6. **Every claim gets an object, a number, or a date.** Adjectives don't carry weight
   here, specifics do. "It took a while" becomes "three months." "It did well" becomes
   show the screen.
7. **Don't chop it into staccato.** "One offer. Zero clients. That's it." is its own AI
   tell. The 4–9 word target is about clauses, so chain them into real sentences with
   and, so, because, which is why. And vary the length hard. (Section 5.)

## 2. Voice fingerprint (how Listy actually talks)

All of this comes from his own words, the journal plus his voice corrections. These
patterns are what separate "sounds like him" from "sounds like the generic builder guy."

- **Statement, then the complication.** His natural unit is a declaration followed by a
  but, so, or except. The complication is the honest part. "Once I start, it rolls. But
  starting is hard." Use the declaration as the hook and the complication as the re-hook.
- **He names the loop in real time.** He steps back and labels the pattern he's stuck in
  while he's still inside it. "It's looping." "My stupid brain delays it to the perfect
  time. Which never comes." This is his strongest move, the mid-video pull-back where he
  names the exact thing the viewer is stuck in too.
- **He corrects himself mid-sentence, not after.** The reasoning gets revised inside the
  sentence. "I think I'm avoiding failure. Not sure that's the real reason." Write the
  confident version, then add one short deflating clause. One. Not a paragraph of hedging.
- **"It feels like" is a specific tool, not filler.** He says it when he doesn't fully
  understand something yet. When he actually has a theory, he says "I think." Don't swap
  one for the other.
- **Dry self-criticism, then the mechanic.** "My brain does this. Here's what I do about
  it." Never "it's been such a hard journey." Quick, dry, move on.
- **Analogy through lived systems, not metaphor.** Show the concrete thing first, then
  label it. "Miss 7am, wait for 7:30, then 8, then tomorrow. ADHD logic."
- **Numbers ground a cost or a decision, never status.** The number attaches to the
  object, not to him. "LalaFun, the busy one." Never "my 130-juta restaurant."
- **Doubt without retreat.** He says the fear, sizes it, and keeps walking. "Worst case?
  One more failure in the bucket." The fear gets named and measured, then walked past. It
  never cancels the action.
- **His hedges mean something.** "Basically," "kind of," "pretty much" mark an
  approximation, so keep them on actual approximations. Used right, they make him sound
  honest instead of unsure.

### Sounds like him / doesn't

| Sounds like him | Does NOT sound like him |
|---|---|
| "Once I start, it rolls. But starting is hard." | "The key is building momentum through habit." |
| "Marketing first. There's no other way." | "I realized I needed to prioritize go-to-market." |
| "We were productive in the MikroFarm era." | "We achieved exceptional output that chapter." |
| "Worst case, one more failure in the bucket." | "Every setback is a stepping stone." |
| "Same energy, different surface." | "It was a complex interplay of factors." |

On pronouns, aim for roughly 70% I, 20% we (the team, staff, GF), 10% you. And "you" only
shows up at the very end, as a small invitation. Never as instruction.

## 3. Structure & pacing (the 45s spine)

Default length is 45s, which is about 95–130 words. A 30s script runs 65–75 words, a 60s
one 130–145.

| Beat | Time | What it does |
|------|------|--------------|
| **Hook** | 0–3s | A claim, a number on screen, or the thing itself. Open a loop. Face, tight. |
| **Context** | 3–8s | Why it matters / the situation. Cut to B-roll, voice continues. |
| **Body A** | 8–15s | First concrete proof point. Not abstract. |
| **Re-hook** | 15–17s | "But here's where it broke." Snap to face ~2s. Fights the 15s cliff. |
| **Body B** | 17–28s | Second proof / the twist. Different B-roll than A. |
| **Re-hook 2** | 28–32s | Face return ~2–3s, slower. Sets up the resolve. Fights the 30s drift. |
| **Payoff** | 32–40s | Resolve the opening loop. One concrete line. |
| **Close** | 40–45s | Stop on the last real thing. No crafted button. (Section 6.) |

A few things the beat sheet can't show on its own:

- Open a loop in the first sentence and don't resolve it until about 70–80% through. If
  the hook gives away the payoff there's no loop, and they're gone by second 8.
- The pacing envelope goes rapid, then slow, then rapid. Tight cuts from 0–8s, let the
  proof breathe from 8–28s with 2–3 second holds, then bring the energy back for the last
  stretch. New creators hold intro-energy the whole way through and it's exhausting to
  watch.
- Re-hooks have to carry new information. A face-return at 30s that says nothing makes
  the viewer feel tricked.

## 4. The numbers rule (read this twice)

This is the rule that gets broken the most, and it's also the one Listy cares about most.
Hence, twice.

- Rupiah and revenue never appear in the spoken track. They live on screen as a proof
  shot. A POS day-total, a Stripe chart, GoFood orders, a bank notification, with the key
  number circled. The voice keeps talking underneath and never announces the figure.
- In the voice, scale gets words, not digits. "My busiest shop." "The small one." "The
  kitchen that supplies the other two." Never "my 130-juta restaurant."
- The only spoken numbers are counts and dates. "Fourteen people." "1,200 plates a day."
  "Three months." "Closed it on the 7th." "I've got three days." Those don't read as flex.
- When a number has to be shown, frame it as a cost or a responsibility, not a win. Pair
  it with payroll, rent, tax. "This pays 14 people" lands. "We did 130 juta" flexes. Let
  the viewer do the bragging in the comments.
- Round down, never up. "About 130," not "131.4." And on screen, real and slightly messy
  beats a clean mocked PNG. Other tabs visible, a real timestamp. A screenshot with no
  visible source now reads as negative proof.

## 5. Spoken register (write for the mouth, not the page)

The basic unit of viral speech is a 2–7 word clause. But those clauses get chained into
real sentences, and both halves matter. Short clauses, chained naturally.

- Contract everything. It's, I'm, don't, that's, here's. Nobody says "do not" out loud.
- Open sentences with And, So, But, Now. That's about 15% of real sentence starts, and it
  does the job "furthermore" and "however" do in writing, minus the stiffness.
- Zero formal connectors. However, therefore, furthermore, moreover, thus, hence,
  nevertheless, in conclusion. Across 14,000 words of real viral speech these show up
  exactly zero times. Use but, so, and, now instead.
- Chain, then snap. Run one long messy clause-chain ("...to the point that I hired
  someone whose whole job is just X"), then cut to something short. Vary the length hard.
  A script where every line is a clipped fragment is just as wrong as one that's all
  run-ons.
- Active voice, always. "Early investors outperform," not "it has been shown that..."
- One rhetorical question max, placed right before the key point or at the hook. Then
  answer it, kill the fear or admit the loop. Never leave it hanging for drama.
- Light discourse markers are welcome. Honestly, look, basically, kind of, pretty much,
  man. And one internet-voice phrase per video, max (it just works / nobody cares / ship
  it).

## 6. Endings (the make-or-break)

Listy's #1 complaint about drafts is closes that "try to make a point." He's right, they
read as AI. Here's what's actually going wrong.

An AI close is on-the-nose. It says the moral out loud and hands it to the viewer. Real
people almost never button a story with its own thesis, so when a script does, it stops
sounding like a person.

The first-choice move is to not write a close at all. End on the last real thing that
happens, say it plainly, stop. Even a "natural-sounding" button like "so I started on a
Thursday" is still writing a close, and it reads as AI. If the final line feels slightly
unfinished, you did it right.

If you do shape an exit, pick exactly one of these six. None of them states a lesson.

1. **End on the concrete detail.** Stop on the physical thing and let the meaning sit
   underneath. "So that's my week. Writing down exactly how much salt goes in the broth."
2. **Undercut / deflate.** Puncture your own seriousness. "Anyway. Back to it." · "I'll
   probably still mess it up."
3. **Trail off.** "We'll see if two new cooks can read my handwriting." · "Ask me in
   December."
4. **Hard-stop on a fact.** End on a concrete figure with zero commentary. A count or a
   date, never spoken rupiah. "Last day's the 11th. I've got a notebook and three days."
5. **Literal callback.** Loop back to the opening image and say it flat. Don't reword it
   into a metaphor. "Back to the same recipe."
6. **Understate it.** Say the smaller, flatter version and let the weight stay implied.
   "Cheaper than the next one would've been."

**Banned closes (these read as AI every single time):**

- "It's not X, it's Y."
- "I'd rather [X] than [Y]." (the symmetric trade-off)
- Stating the theme: "a business shouldn't live in one person's head."
- The matched-pair aphorism: "The folder remembers. I just decide."
- Ending on an abstract noun: "That's the bet." "That's discipline." "That's the
  timestamp."
- Any line built to be screenshotted on a motivational graphic. If it'd look at home on a
  poster, kill it.

The test is simple. Read the close out loud and ask: is it stating the lesson, or ending
on a thing? If you can hear the moral being announced, rewrite it to a concrete detail, an
undercut, or an unfinished thread.

## 7. Hooks (first 3 seconds)

Open on a scene or a claim. Never a logo, never an intro card, never "hey guys." The hook
ships in 3 seconds or less, and it's specific, not vague. Write at least 5 hook variants
per script, then pick one.

The hook types (these match `hook_type` in the frontmatter):

- **receipts** — number/result first, proof shot in seconds 2–4. The number goes on SCREEN.
- **build-reveal** — "I built X in Y." Cut to the working thing.
- **contrarian** — a take you've lived the scar tissue for.
- **curiosity** — a specific promise with the payoff withheld. The payoff has to be a real
  tactic or number.
- **pain-mirror** — name a private fear out loud, then show the fix from your own build.
- **fail-story** — a public loss with a date and a number. Unfakeable for an operator.
- **bts** — open on the actual screen or kitchen, not your face.
- **listicle** — 3–5 items only, tactical, fast cuts.
- **hot-take** — a category-specific opinion you can defend through the whole video.
- **before-after** — a visual delta (dashboard, design, day-1 vs day-30).

And one rule for the camera: show face when the hook is a claim. Cut to the screen when
the hook is proof.

## 8. B-roll & proof (the operator's unfair advantage)

Something on screen changes every 1.5–3s. And every B-roll clip earns its place by proving
a claim. If it doesn't prove or punctuate, cut it.

Listy's moat is that he has both dashboards and a physical ops floor. Cut between a Stripe
chart and a wok firing in the same 30 seconds, because that contrast is the brand. Shoot
both worlds.

The B-roll vocabulary, and what each shot says:

- **screenshare / code** says "I built this"
- **POS / metrics** says authority (keep it 1–2s)
- **product-in-hand** says "this is real"
- **kitchen / ops / staff** says "I run things." This one's the moat.
- **phone-UI** says customer experience
- **whiteboard** says "I think in systems"
- **throwbacks** say "I earned the right"

Translate the restaurant proof for a tech audience. The POS daily report is "this is our
Stripe." The 7pm queue is the hockey stick. Repeat-order receipts are the retention
cohort. 14 staff at briefing is the team page. Say it out loud: "You ship code. I ship
1,200 plates a day. Same loop."

The proof-shot pattern: mid-video the talking head shrinks or freezes, a real screenshot
slams full-frame with the key number circled, hold it 1.5–2s, back to face. Land it on a
claim, not a tangent. One key number, circled, audio still talking underneath. Max two per
video.

## 9. Captions & on-screen text

- Default style is sentence-chunked 3–5 word phrases, which fits builder content. Karaoke
  word-by-word only for a hype beat. One pinned top line restates the hook and holds for
  5–10s.
- One clean sans font (Inter / Geist / SF Pro) with locked weight, size and stroke, reused
  every video. Mono or tabular figures for numbers so the digits don't jitter. One accent
  color for highlights. No emojis, no rainbow, no platform-default preset.
- Highlight 1–2 words per phrase. Nouns, numbers, names, action verbs. Never articles.
- Numbers are a design element. Count-up on the first reveal only, pair the number with a
  unit chip ("3 **restaurants**"), and hold it 1.5–2s so it can be screenshotted.
- Bilingual setup: English captions always, since the builder audience is international.
  Indonesian VO is fine, just burn English captions and let the accent be texture. Keep
  dish, place and brand names in Indonesian, untranslated (*ayam geprek*, *Kediri*,
  *warung*).

## 10. CTA

Default is no hard CTA at all. The work is the CTA, and if the last frame already provokes
"who is this guy," shut up.

If you do use one, make it soft, conditional and identity-based, and put it after the
payoff. "If you build things on weekends, this account's for you." Never "follow now for
more dev tips." One CTA max, and never read it louder than the rest.

Also, run the CTA as burned-in text during the payoff, not as a spoken last line. A spoken
CTA breaks the loop.

## 11. ESL delivery constraints (write words his mouth can land cleanly)

The accent is Indonesian-English. Pick words that record clean on the first take.

- Avoid final consonant clusters when a synonym exists. Prefer "the price" over "the
  costs," "find" over "find out," "think about" over "think through." Prefer "exactly"
  over "exact," and "we shipped it" over ending a sentence on "shipped."
- Avoid v/f/b pile-ups in one clause. "Proven very profitable" is a tongue trap, break it
  up. Proper nouns starting with B, P or F sit alone, not next to similar sounds.
- Avoid formal words of 4+ syllables. Systematically, fundamentally, predominantly,
  nevertheless. Use "mostly," "a lot," "still."
- Easy swaps: clean/solid instead of perfectly. Just/exactly instead of specifically.
  Making money / covers cost instead of profitable. Show / here's the number instead of
  prove. Honestly/look instead of frankly. Hard / the problem is instead of frustrating.
- Keep sentences short enough to deliver with correct stress. Mark the one stressed word
  per sentence, and allow one high-effort word per sentence, max.

## 12. The gut-check (run before calling a script done)

Read the whole thing out loud at talking speed. Then go down the list:

1. **Stranger test.** Would this make sense to someone who's never met him? No insider
   names without a one-line gloss.
2. **Numbers.** Is any rupiah or revenue figure spoken? Move it to the screen. And are
   the spoken numbers only counts and dates?
3. **The close.** Is it ending on a thing, or announcing a lesson? Could it work as a
   poster caption? If yes, kill it.
4. **Staccato check.** Are there 3+ clipped fragments in a row? Chain some into real
   sentences.
5. **AI tells.** Em dashes, "it's not X it's Y," perfect triplets, essay transitions,
   guru words. Purge them.
6. **Specificity.** Does every claim have an object, a number, or a date?
7. **The Listy test.** Does it sound like the man in the journal, or like something more
   polished and abstract? If it's more polished, cut until it sounds like him.
8. **Loop.** Does the hook open something the payoff closes? If the hook gives it away,
   rewrite.

Passes all 8? It's ready to shoot.

## 13. A worked example (the target)

**Brief:** failure pillar, fail-story hook, 45s. He closed a satellite restaurant (SaraFun).

**Hooks (5, picked #2):**
1. I closed a restaurant this year. Here's the part nobody posts.
2. *(chosen)* I shut one of my shops down in April. Best money I didn't keep spending.
3. Everyone says don't quit. I quit a whole restaurant. On purpose.
4. This shop was open four months. Then I killed it.
5. The mistake wasn't opening it. It was opening it without one guy.

**Voiceover:**
> I shut one of my shops down in April. (beat) Four months open. Then I closed it.
> Not because it lost money. Because I opened it before I had the one person who runs a
> kitchen when I'm not there. So every problem came straight to my phone. Supplier's late,
> my phone. Cook quits, my phone. I was running it from a different city, which, yeah,
> that's on me. The main shop was fine the whole time, because that one *has* the guy. So
> I added it up, and closing was cheaper than keeping it on life support. Two cooks from
> the closed one moved to the main kitchen. They start Monday.

**On-screen:** pinned "Closed a shop. On purpose." · proof shot = the closure math /
final P&L with the number circled (NOT spoken) · throwback photo of the shop's day-1.

**Why it works:** the number lives on screen, and the voice only carries counts and dates
("four months," "April," "Monday"). The loop ("best money I didn't keep spending")
resolves at "cheaper than keeping it on life support." And the close ends on a concrete
fact, two cooks starting Monday, not a lesson. No "the real lesson is hire first." The
viewer assembles that themselves.

**What would've made it AI-y:** ending on "Don't open the second shop until the kitchen
can run without you." True, but on-the-nose. The script *shows* it and stops.
