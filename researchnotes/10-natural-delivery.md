# Research: Writing for Natural Spoken Delivery
## Focus: Short-Form Video (TikTok/Reels) — Non-Native English Speaker

**Date:** 2026-06-07
**Scope:** How to write scripts that sound like real speech when recorded, with specific adaptations for Indonesian-accented English delivery.

---

## 1. "Write Like You Talk" — Core Techniques

### The Talk-First Method
The most reliable technique for natural-sounding scripts is to generate them through speech, not typing. Record yourself explaining the concept verbally — voice memo, voice note, or any speech-to-text tool — then clean the transcript. The output naturally includes sentence rhythms, breath patterns, and word choices you would actually use on camera. Typing tends to produce more formal, symmetrical constructions that nobody speaks aloud.

Sources: Conversational writing guides at Voice123, Voices.com, Resemble AI

### The Read-Aloud Test
After drafting, read the entire script out loud at recording pace. Anything that makes you stumble, pause unnaturally, or requires a re-read is a rewrite flag. This is not optional polish — it is the primary editing pass. The rule: if it sounds wrong spoken, it is wrong, full stop. A script that reads well on the page but trips you up on camera is a bad script.

Sources: Celtx Blog, Castos Podcast Script guide, TechSmith script guide

### Voice Memo Drafting Workflow
Practical workflow used by YouTube creators and podcasters:
1. Record a loose voice memo explaining the topic as if to a friend.
2. Transcribe it (Otter.ai, Descript, or phone transcription).
3. Clean the transcript: remove heavy fillers ("um", repeated false starts), keep light ones that sound natural.
4. That cleaned transcript is your first draft. Adjust for concision, not for formality.

The key insight: transcribed speech already has natural sentence lengths, natural breath units, and conversational vocabulary. Starting from typed prose and trying to "loosen it up" is harder and produces worse results.

Sources: MakeUseOf voice-to-writing guide, Speechify voice-over guide

### Contractions Are Non-Negotiable
"Do not" → "don't." "You are" → "you're." "It is" → "it's." No exceptions in short-form video scripts. Every expanded contraction reads as formal and signals reading, not talking. Run a find-replace pass as a standard editing step.

### Sentence Length Target
Aim for sentences under 12 words as the default. Allow occasional longer sentences for rhythm variation, but flag anything over 18 words for rewriting. Short-form video moves at 2–3 words per second spoken naturally. Long sentences make the camera feel slow even when the content is dense.

## 2. Why AI-Written Scripts Sound Stiff — Known Tells and Fixes

### Em Dash Overuse
AI models (ChatGPT in particular) use em dashes at roughly 3–5x the rate of human writers because their training data skews toward academic and journalistic text where em dashes signal sophistication. In spoken scripts, the em dash is invisible — it becomes an awkward pause or a run-on, depending on how the speaker reads it. Fix: replace every em dash with a period, a comma, or a rewrite that breaks the thought into two sentences.

Sources: The Nerd Daily on em dash epidemic, Medium / Brent Csutoras em dash analysis, Plagiarism Today

### "It's Not X, It's Y" Parallelism
AI over-uses contrast constructions: "It's not about the money, it's about the mission." "It's not what you do, it's how you do it." These have a formal, debate-team cadence that no human sustains for more than one instance. In short-form video they signal rehearsed corporate speech, not a real person talking. Fix: use this construction at most once per video, for genuine rhetorical emphasis only.

Sources: PR Daily on AI writing tells, Woodard report on AI-generated writing signs

### Perfect Parallelism
AI defaults to lists where every item has identical grammatical structure and approximately equal length: "First, you need to do X. Second, you need to do Y. Third, you need to do Z." Human speech is messier — items vary in length, some get asides, some get emphasis. Fix: vary item length deliberately. Let one item run long with an example. Let another be a single punchy word.

### Over-Polished Transitions
"With that in mind..." / "Now that we've established..." / "Moving on to..." / "It's worth noting that..." — these are writing transitions, not speech transitions. Human speech transitions are simpler: "OK so..." / "And here's the thing..." / "Right, so..." / "Which brings me to..." Fix: replace any transition that you would only see in a written essay.

### Formulaic Rhythm
AI produces scripts where almost every sentence lands on the same stress beat. This creates a monotone reading cadence even when the words vary. Fix: after drafting, read aloud and deliberately vary where you place emphasis. Rewrite sentences so natural stress falls on different positions across consecutive sentences.

Sources: PlainTextConverter on AI em dash patterns, Alibaba product insights on AI dialogue

## 3. Conversational Devices That Survive Recording

These are techniques that feel loose or "imperfect" on the page but read as human and engaged on camera. They are not errors — they are signals.

### Controlled False Starts
A single false start per video, when done intentionally, reads as authentic real-time thinking: "So I was going to say — actually, the better way to put it is..." Only works when it clearly signals a course correction, not when it sounds like a mistake. Scripted false starts must be short; anything over 5 words sounds rehearsed.

### Asides and Parenthetical Remarks
A brief aside breaks the lecture cadence: "...which, by the way, most people skip entirely..." These signal that the speaker is thinking as they talk, not reciting. Limit to 1–2 per video. Longer asides compound and slow pace.

### Direct Second-Person Checks
"You know what I mean?" / "Right?" / "See what I'm saying?" / "Does that make sense?" — these invite the viewer into the conversation. One per video maximum. They signal the speaker cares whether the point landed, which is a humanizing beat. Avoid back-to-back in the same script.

### Self-Corrections and Reframes
"Actually, let me put it differently..." or "No wait, here's a better way to think about it..." These are harder to execute on camera without looking uncertain, but when practiced they are powerful authenticity signals. Script them only for moments where you are genuinely pivoting to a clearer framing.

### Concrete Specificity Over Abstract Claims
Human speech anchors abstractions immediately: "It costs a lot" → "It runs about 2 million rupiah." "It takes time" → "I'm talking 3 months minimum." AI scripts tend to keep claims abstract. Specificity is a conversational tell — it signals lived experience.

Sources: Celtx YouTube script guide, ScriptStorm conversational video guide, Riverside podcast script guide

## 4. ESL-Specific Considerations — Indonesian-Accent English Delivery

### The Phonological Gaps to Script Around
Indonesian lacks several English phonemes. When these appear in a script they create delivery hesitation or mispronunciation that breaks the viewer's attention. The most common problem phonemes for Indonesian EFL speakers:
- /v/ — often realized as /f/: "very" → "fery"
- /θ/ (th- unvoiced) — "think", "three", "through" — often realized as /t/ or /d/
- /ð/ (th- voiced) — "the", "this", "that" — often realized as /d/
- /dʒ/ (j as in "just") — not always smooth
- Final consonant clusters — "strengths", "texts", "costs" → cluster simplification is natural

**Script fix:** Avoid words with final consonant clusters where a synonym exists. Prefer "the price is..." over "the costs are..." Prefer "find" over "find out" before a word starting with a consonant. Prefer "think about" over "think through."

Source: Academic papers on Indonesian EFL consonant clusters; MDPI Pronunciation Features of Indonesian-Accented English; ResearchGate papers on final consonant cluster simplification

### Aspiration Mismatch
English aspirates /p/, /t/, /k/ at the start of stressed syllables (the puff of air in "pit" vs "spit"). Indonesian does not aspirate these consonants. On camera this creates an accent marker. Script fix: not avoidable through word choice, but awareness matters — when practicing delivery, consciously aspirate initial stop consonants in key words ("point", "take", "keep").

Source: Indonesian phonology guides, aspiration analysis papers (ResearchGate)

### Stress-Timing vs. Syllable-Timing
English is stress-timed: stressed syllables recur at roughly equal intervals, regardless of how many unstressed syllables fall between them. Indonesian is syllable-timed: every syllable gets approximately equal duration. Indonesian speakers reading English scripts often produce syllable-timed output, which sounds flat and monotonous to native English ears.

**Script fix for writers:** Mark stress on the key word in each sentence. Example: "The REASON most people fail..." not "The reason most people fail..." When recording, deliver the stressed word with more energy, not just more volume. Duration and pitch carry more than loudness.

**Script design fix:** Write shorter breath units. A sentence of 8–10 words is easier to deliver with correct stress-timing than a 16-word sentence. Shorter sentences allow the speaker to reset between stress patterns.

Source: Academic papers on stress-timed vs syllable-timed rhythm in Indonesian EFL learners

### Avoid Polysyllabic Formal Words
Words like "predominantly", "substantially", "nevertheless", "notwithstanding" are hard to stress correctly and slow delivery. In short-form video they also feel formal. Prefer shorter equivalents: "mostly", "a lot", "still", "even so." This serves both naturalness and ESL delivery confidence.

### The Penultimate Stress Trap
Indonesian defaults to stress on the second-to-last syllable. Some English words share this pattern ("important", "remember", "together") and are easy to deliver. Words where English stress falls on the first or third syllable are harder: "camera" (CAM-era, not cam-ERA), "calculate" (CAL-culate), "absolute" (AB-solute). Avoid these words in scripts or flag them for practice before recording.

### Practical ESL Delivery Tips from Duarte and Public Speaking Research
- Over-articulate during recording — form each word carefully, as if the listener cannot hear perfectly. This compensates for the native-listener's unfamiliarity with a specific accent and improves clarity without changing the accent.
- Slow down 10–15% below your natural conversational pace when recording. This gives stress-timing room to emerge.
- Script the opening and closing verbatim, deliver the middle from bullet points. The two high-anxiety moments (start and finish) benefit from memorized delivery; the middle benefits from natural improvisation within a structure.
- Use personal stories and specific examples — these allow the speaker to draw on natural Indonesian speech rhythms rather than forcing formal English cadence.

Sources: Duarte ESL speakers guide, Public Speaking Skills for Non-native Speakers (CUNY Pressbooks), Presentationprep.com non-native guide

## 5. Talk Track vs. Verbatim Script — When to Use Each

### The Core Distinction
- **Verbatim script:** Every word written out. Delivery requires memorization or a teleprompter.
- **Bullet-point scaffold (talk track):** 5–10 key points, each a short phrase. Actual words generated in the moment.
- **Hybrid:** Opening line scripted verbatim, middle as bullets, closing line scripted verbatim.

### When Verbatim Works
- Complex technical explanations where exact wording matters.
- Statistics, prices, specific claims that must be accurate.
- Openings and closings where phrasing has been refined for impact.
- Short-form video under 30 seconds that has been fully memorized (memorized ≠ read on camera).

The failure mode of verbatim: reading on camera is visible. Eye movement, rhythmic pacing, and the slight pause at line breaks all signal "reading." The only way to use a verbatim script without looking like you're reading is to fully memorize it — which requires significant rehearsal time.

### When Bullet Points Win
- Content you know deeply from lived experience (your own business, your own process).
- Videos where personality and spontaneity are the product (vlogs, opinions, reactions).
- Camera-shy creators who freeze when they lose their place in a script — bullet points allow recovery without starting over.
- Short-form video in general: the intimacy and connection TikTok/Reels reward is much harder to generate when reading verbatim.

Recommendation from short-form practitioners: for a 60-second video, write 7–10 bullets (approximately 150–200 words at natural pace). Hook bullet can be a full scripted sentence. Closing bullet can be a full scripted sentence. Everything between: free delivery within the framework.

### The Hybrid Structure for ESL Camera-Shy Creators
This approach is specifically suited to Listy's situation:

1. **Scripted hook** (1–2 sentences, memorized): The first impression is too important to improvise. Write this exactly, then rehearse until it comes out clean without reading.
2. **Bullet middle** (3–5 points): Each bullet is a topic, not a sentence. Example: "explain the 3-month cycle" not "The cycle takes 3 months because..."
3. **Scripted close** (1 sentence + CTA): Same as the hook — memorize this exactly.

This structure means the two moments where viewers decide to stay or scroll are fully controlled. The middle, where the speaker has deep knowledge, is delivered naturally.

Sources: Teleprompter.com short-form video strategy, TikTok/@copyposse creator advice, Teleprompterpro.com scripted vs unscripted analysis, Camera shy tips from Natasha Lane Design, fewstones.com camera-shy presenter tips

## De-Stiffening Checklist (12–15 items)

Apply this to any script before recording:

1. **Contraction check** — Find every "do not", "you are", "it is", "we are", "they are" and replace with contractions. No exceptions.
2. **Em dash purge** — Find every em dash. Replace with a period or comma. Rewrite if neither works.
3. **"It's not X, it's Y" count** — Allow maximum 1 per video. Delete the rest.
4. **Transition audit** — Flag any transition that would appear in an essay ("with that in mind", "moving on to", "it's worth noting"). Replace with spoken equivalents or cut entirely.
5. **Sentence length** — Flag every sentence over 15 words. Rewrite or split.
6. **Parallel list variation** — If you have a list of 3+ items, deliberately vary the length and structure of at least one item.
7. **Abstract claims check** — Every abstract claim ("it takes time", "it costs a lot") needs a specific anchor ("about 3 months", "around 1.5 million rupiah").
8. **Second-person moment** — Add one direct check: "you know what I mean?" or "right?" Maximum one per video.
9. **Read-aloud test** — Read the entire script out loud at recording pace. Mark every stumble. Rewrite those lines.
10. **Final consonant cluster scan** — Check for words ending in cluster consonants (strengths, texts, costs, asks). Replace where a synonym exists.
11. **Polysyllabic word scan** — Replace any word over 4 syllables with a shorter equivalent unless precision requires it.
12. **Stress marker pass** — Identify the key word in each sentence. If the script has 3 consecutive sentences where the stressed word falls in the same position, reorder or rewrite one.
13. **Opening memorization test** — Can you deliver the first two sentences without looking at the script? If not, shorten until you can.
14. **Closing memorization test** — Same as above for the last two sentences.
15. **Voice memo verification** — Record a voice memo of yourself delivering the full script from memory or bullets. Play it back. Does it sound like you talking to someone, or does it sound like a corporate announcement? If the latter, find the stiff section and rewrite it.
