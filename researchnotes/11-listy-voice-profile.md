## Part 1 — Voice Fingerprint

### Pattern 1: Correction mid-sentence, not at the end

He revises his own reasoning while still in the sentence. The correction is the honest part.

> "I think I'm avoiding unnecessary acts of practice that will end up in failure. Not sure if that's the correct reason." [J]

> "That might be alibi." [J]

He does not polish before speaking. The self-doubt lands inside the sentence, not as a followup. In scripts, this translates to: write the confident version, then add one deflating clause. Not a long qualifier — one phrase.

---

### Pattern 2: Analogy through lived systems, not metaphor

When he explains an abstract pattern, he reaches for a concrete operational comparison.

> "PC gaming is itself a really hard game. Same energy, different surface." [J]

> "If I miss a morning slot, my stupid brain delays it to the 'perfect time' later. Which never comes." [J]

He does not say "procrastination works like a ratchet." He says what actually happens, then names what it is. Scripts should follow: show the thing, then label it. Not the other way around.

---

### Pattern 3: Short declarative followed by the complication

His natural sentence structure is statement + but/so/except. He rarely leaves a declaration standing alone.

> "Once I start practicing, it kind of rolls — but starting is hard." [J]

> "I love experiments, but this one is not a physical experiment." [J]

> "I actually enjoy the practice — I just don't know why I don't start." [J]

The complication is what he actually thinks. The statement is setup. In scripts: use the declaration as the hook, the complication as the re-hook.

---

### Pattern 4: Numbers used to ground cost, not to brag

When he mentions numbers, they appear to frame a tradeoff or a decision, not as a status signal.

> "180k/month. 3x/week = 12k/session. 5x/week = bare minimum to make it worth it." [J]

> "20–30 min when I actually practice." [J]

He never says "I run a 130M restaurant." He says "LalaFun, 130M/mo" — the number is attached to the object, not to him. In scripts: numbers describe the situation, not the person.

---

### Pattern 5: Naming the loop he is in, in real time

He is unusually aware of his own patterns as they are happening.

> "How do I start practice? I can't even do that yet." [J]

> "It's looping." [J]

> "I have so many failures in the past, and it feels like I cannot fail again." [J]

This is a native habit: stepping back and labeling the dynamic while inside it. In scripts, this is his strongest move — the mid-video pull-back where he names the pattern the viewer is also stuck in. It feels honest because it is.

---

### Pattern 6: "It feels like" as his honest-signal prefix

He uses "it feels like" specifically when he is reporting something he does not fully understand yet, distinguishing it from things he knows.

> "It feels like this will fail." [J]

> "It feels like I cannot fail again." [J]

> "It feels like I'm trying to do something really hard." [J]

This is not hedging. This is him marking the difference between analysis and experience. In scripts: do not substitute "it feels like" with "I think." They mean different things for him. Reserve "it feels like" for the emotional honesty beat; use "I think" when he has a theory.

---

### Pattern 7: "Stupid" as self-directed, not self-pitying

He calls his own behavior stupid without dramatizing it. It reads like a technical descriptor.

> "If I miss a morning slot, my stupid brain delays it to the 'perfect time' later." [J]

> "ADHD-style logic." [J]

He is not asking for sympathy. He is describing a mechanism. In scripts: self-directed criticism should be quick, dry, and followed immediately by the mechanic. Never dwell. "My brain does this — here's what I do about it" not "it's been such a hard journey."

---

### Pattern 8: Parallel structure in reasoning, not in rhetoric

He naturally reasons by comparison — not to sound balanced, but because that is how he processes.

> "I grew up in a tangible-productivity family. I see physical things as productive. Social media is new for me — I only started posting 2 years ago, only changed my WhatsApp profile photo 2 years ago." [J]

> "Same energy, different surface." [J]

His parallelisms feel discovered, not constructed. In scripts: if you write a comparison, make sure both sides of it are earned from his actual situation — not inserted for rhetorical symmetry.

---

### Pattern 9: "That's the timestamp" — pinning to a moment, not a narrative

He dates his reasoning without building a story arc around it. The timestamp is evidence, not drama.

> "We were really productive during MikroFarm era. The gear slowed down when I let gaming back into the routine. That's the timestamp." [J]

In scripts: when referencing turning points, give the date or event name, then move on. No origin-story narration. The reference should feel like a log entry, not a flashback.

---

### Pattern 10: One-word sentences and question fragments as pivots

He punctuates reasoning with very short answers that double as pivots into the next thought.

> "Test." [J] (entire answer to "launch or test?")

> "It's looping." [J]

> "Marketing first." [J]

These are not rhetorical stabs. They are genuine compressed conclusions. In scripts, these belong at structural pivots — hook-to-body, body-to-payoff. One word or one clause to declare the verdict before moving on.

---

### Pattern 11: Doubt without retreat

He articulates doubt but does not use it to cancel the action. The fear is stated, then the obligation continues anyway.

> "Really nothing. Me being less confident, or just one more failure in the bucket." [J] (on worst case of a bad launch)

> "Views will flatline for the next 6 months — I know that." [J]

This is the clearest register of who he is on camera: someone who has counted the cost and kept going. In scripts, the "worst case" moment should always resolve this way — named, measured, then continued past.

---

### Pattern 12: English grammar patterns from Indonesian baseline [INF]

This is inference, not a direct quote. Based on his sentence structures in the journal (written in English, likely via voice-to-text or direct typing):

- Subject-verb-object is clean. He does not invert.
- Articles (a, an, the) appear inconsistently — he sometimes drops them: "I'm trying to do something really hard" vs potential drop in faster speech.
- He uses "really" as an intensifier more than "very": "really hard," "really nothing," "really productive."
- He uses "just" as a minimizer/pivot: "I just don't know why I don't start."
- Conditional structures are present but simple: "if it were a physical experiment, I could iterate."
- He avoids subjunctive-heavy constructions. Keep scripts in the indicative.

---

### Pattern 13: Rhetorical question, then immediate self-answer [INF]

Inferred from: Q9/Q10 in the journal, where he asks himself the question and collapses the fear before it can settle.

> "What scares me about June 1? That I won't deliver good stuff. But we already established the end product doesn't matter yet." [J]

> "How do I start practice? I can't even do that yet." [J]

In one case the self-answer dissolves the fear; in the other it admits the loop. In scripts: rhetorical questions should follow his pattern — either kill the fear in the next sentence, or admit the loop. Never leave the question hanging for drama.

---

### Pattern 14: "Basically" and "kind of" as precision reducers, not filler [INF]

Inference from accent map and guideline note (section 4: "basically, kinda, pretty much"). He uses hedging adverbs not as filler but as semantic signals that the claim is approximate.

> "it kind of rolls" [J] — meaning: the momentum is real but not absolute

In scripts, these words belong on approximations. Do not scrub them. They make him sound honest, not uncertain.

## Part 3 — Delivery Constraints from Accent Map

Source: feedback_voice_corrections.md

### Sounds / words the TTS or audience may mishear

The voice corrections table reveals which English sounds his accent consistently distorts in voice-to-text. Use this as a proxy for which sounds are high-effort to produce clearly and which words to avoid putting in scripts:

| Distortion pattern | Evidence | Script constraint |
|---|---|---|
| v/f/p confusion | "very" heard as "ferry"; "battery" heard as "ferry" | Avoid piling f/v/b sounds in the same clause. Do not use "perfectly fine" or "prove fairly fast." |
| Labial stop confusion (b/p/f) | "ferry" for "Peri"; "battery" for "Peri" [VC] | Proper nouns starting with B/P/F should appear alone in a sentence, not adjacent to similar sounds. |
| Nasal-to-stop shift | "happy" heard as "Habib" [VC] | Avoid "-py", "-phy" word endings adjacent to proper nouns. |
| Initial consonant cluster reduction [INF] | Likely from Indonesian phonology, which avoids complex onset clusters (str-, scr-, spl-) | Avoid "struck," "screened," "split" as sentence-opener words. Prefer "hit," "showed," "cut." |
| Final consonant weakening [INF] | Common in Indonesian-accented English (final t/d/k/g weaken or drop) | Do not end a key claim word on a stop: "exact," "shipped" → prefer "exactly," "we shipped it." Endings with open vowels are cleaner. |
| r-coloring [INF] | Indonesian r is tapped/trilled, not rhotic | Avoid tongue-twisting r-clusters: "rural," "rarely," "rarely-run," "rarely-rebuilt." One r per content word is fine. |

### Words to prefer over their alternatives

Based on the above patterns:

| Avoid | Prefer |
|---|---|
| "perfectly" | "clean," "solid," "exact" |
| "specifically" | "just," "exactly," "right there" |
| "profitable" | "making money," "covers cost" |
| "failure" (heavy final cluster) | "failed," "it failed," "dead" |
| "productivity" | "output," "getting things done" |
| "frustrating" | "hard," "annoying," "the problem is" |
| "frankly" | "honestly," "look" |
| "prove" | "show," "here's the number," "the receipt" |
| "perfectly fine" | "works," "it held up," "still running" |

### Sound sequences to avoid in the same breath group

These are clustering risks — where two adjacent sounds from the distortion list land in the same phrase and compound the problem:

- b/p + f/v in adjacent words: "proven very profitable" — break it up
- Final stop + initial cluster: "that script starts" — rewrite as "this script, it starts"
- Four-syllable technical words: "systematically," "fundamentally," "intentionally" — cut to two syllables or recast as a phrase

## How This Extends the Existing Voice Profile (section 4, guidelines.md)

Section 4 of `guidelines.md` establishes the archetype (4–9 word sentence units, numbers replace adjectives, first-person past tense, no swearing, operator vocab). This file does not contradict those rules. It adds:

1. The specific verbal habits that produce that archetype for him natively (patterns 1–11 above).
2. The distinction between his hedging as semantic vs filler (pattern 14).
3. The ESL-specific phonological constraints that affect word choice in scripts (part 3).
4. Evidence-grounded examples so future scripts can be tested against real samples, not just the archetype.

When in doubt: read a draft sentence aloud at 135 WPM and ask whether it sounds like something he said in the journal. If it sounds more polished or more abstract, cut it.
