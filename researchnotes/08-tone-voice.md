# Tone, Voice & Narration — Operator/Builder Voice Profile

Reference profile for scripting short-form video in the register of Pieter Levels, Marc Lou, Theo, Ben Lang, Sam Parr, Greg Isenberg, Daniel Vassallo, Tibo, and operator-mode Hormozi. Audience-author: 28yo Indonesian operator, ESL-strong, camera-shy, mostly voiceover.

## 1. Sentence length / cadence
Short stabs dominate. The default unit is 4–9 words. Long sentences only appear when chaining a setup before a punch.

Pattern: `Setup. Setup. Twist.` or `Number. Action. Result.`

- Operator: "Built it in a weekend. Posted it Monday. $400 by Friday."
- Guru (avoid): "Through disciplined execution, I was able to validate a product-market fit signal within the first week of deployment."

Use one comma max per sentence. If you need two, split it. Fragments are fine. Start sentences with `And`, `So`, `But` — it sounds spoken, not written.

## 2. Vocabulary
Jargon density: **low-to-medium**, and always immediately grounded by a number or concrete object.

Safe builder slang (use freely): `ship`, `built`, `launch`, `MRR`, `users`, `stack`, `landing page`, `wrapper`, `pivot`, `kill`, `dead`, `traction`. Pieter and Marc say "ship" constantly.

Treat as red-flag guru words (avoid): `synergy`, `leverage`, `scale to 7 figures`, `unlock`, `transformation`, `journey`, `ecosystem play`, `disruptive`, `value-add`.

Rule of thumb from Vassallo / Levels: every claim should have either a dollar amount, a count, or a date attached. "$300 MRR." "Two restaurants." "Three months in." Numbers do the heavy lifting that adjectives do for gurus.

## 3. Swearing, slang, internet voice
Theo and Sam Parr swear casually (`shit`, `damn`, occasional `fuck`). Pieter rarely swears but uses `man`, `like`, `literally` as filler. Marc Lou almost never swears — his edge comes from speed claims, not language. Hormozi operator clips avoid swearing.

For an ESL Indonesian creator: **don't swear**. It reads as imported, not native. The voice you want is "calm operator," not "shock jock." Lean on Pieter's register: `man`, `honestly`, `basically`, `kinda`, `pretty much`. These cost nothing and humanize the voiceover.

Internet voice that travels: `lol no`, `it just works`, `nobody cares`, `ship it`, `dead simple`, `vibes`, `legit`. Use sparingly — 1 per video max.

## 4. Person framing
Default: **first-person past tense.** "I built." "I tried." "I killed it after two weeks." This is the operator move — you're reporting from the field, not teaching from a stage.

Second person ("you") appears only at the end as a tiny invitation, never as instruction: "if you're running a restaurant, this might save you a month."

Third person ("here's how Pieter built…") is a different genre — explainer/case study. Don't use it for your own work. Use it only when you're genuinely studying someone else.

Ratio target: **70% I / 20% we (team, GF, staff) / 10% you.**

## 5. Confidence markers — matter-of-fact register
The operator asserts by reporting, not by claiming. The verb is `did`, not `will` or `can`.

- Operator: "Three restaurants. One is the cash cow. One is breaking even. One I'm rebuilding."
- Guru: "I've built a portfolio of high-performing F&B assets."

Drop hedges (`I think`, `maybe`, `kind of`) from claims about your own work. Keep them when describing the future. Confidence about the past + humility about the future = trustworthy operator.

Avoid superlatives. "Best ever," "game-changing," "next-level" all read guru. Use specific comparatives instead: "cheaper than our old supplier," "faster than the previous menu."

## 6. Self-deprecation / vulnerability
Admitting a fail strengthens authority when the fail comes with a number and a lesson — not a feeling.

- Strong: "Lost 80M IDR on a satellite outlet. Closed it in four months. Lesson: don't open until the senior cook is hired."
- Weak: "It was such a humbling journey and I learned so much about myself."

Vassallo and Pieter both lead with "this didn't work." Marc Lou's "17 failed projects" line is the template. Open with the loss, close with the mechanic. No therapy talk.

## 7. Operator voice vs Guru voice — side by side

| Topic | Guru | Operator |
|---|---|---|
| Revenue | "Scaled to 7 figures" | "Hit 130M IDR/mo, stalled there" |
| Launch | "Crushed our launch" | "Posted Monday. 12 orders." |
| Failure | "Pivoted strategically" | "Killed it. Wasted 3 months." |
| Process | "Systematized for growth" | "One Google Sheet. One staff. Works." |
| Team | "Built a world-class team" | "Hired two cooks. Trained them in a week." |
| Advice | "You need to dream bigger" | "Don't open the second outlet yet." |

Operator voice always grounds in object, number, or time. Guru voice abstracts away from them.

## 8. Indonesian-creator-in-English considerations
- **Accent: keep it.** Pieter (Dutch), Marc (French), Tibo (French) — accents are credibility markers, not problems. Don't fake American intonation.
- **Don't force slang.** No `bro`, `dude`, `y'all`, `lowkey`, `no cap`, `bet`. They date fast and read costume.
- **Code-switch only for proper nouns.** Names of dishes, places, businesses stay Indonesian: *ayam geprek*, *Kediri*, *LalaFun*, *warung*. Don't translate them; let them sit. This is authentic, not foreign.
- **Avoid translated idioms.** "Make a bridge," "open the road" — translate to plain English: "set it up," "get started."
- **Short sentences favor ESL.** They reduce grammar load and increase clarity. Use the limitation as a style.
- **Pronunciation prep:** mark hard words in the script phonetically before recording. One word per sentence at most should be high-effort.

## 9. Voiceover pace (WPM)
Operator-channel short-form sits at **140–155 WPM**. Hormozi-style cut-heavy edits push 160+ but only because filler is removed in post. Pieter on podcasts runs ~130 WPM (conversational). Marc Lou demo videos run ~150.

For an ESL voiceover, target **135–145 WPM** in the raw take, then tighten in edit. This:
- Keeps articulation clean
- Leaves room for B-roll beats
- Matches the "thoughtful operator" register over "hyper hustler"

Script word counts:
- 30s short: 65–75 words
- 60s short: 130–145 words
- 90s short: 195–215 words

Read it aloud once before recording. If you stumble twice on the same sentence, rewrite it shorter.

---

**Default opening moves** (steal these):
- "I run [X]. Here's the part nobody posts."
- "Tried [X] for [N] months. Killed it. Here's why."
- "$[N] in. $[N] out. This is what worked."
- "Most people get [X] wrong. I did too."

**Default closing moves:**
- "That's it. Back to work."
- "Might break next month. We'll see."
- "If you're [X], this might save you [Y]."
- No CTAs in the Hormozi-influencer sense. The work is the CTA.
